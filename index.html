Adjustable Bike Pedaling System for Knee Rehab

For my final year capstone project, my group and I developed a bike pedaling system that was intended to be used in a clinincal setting as well as a research setting on a stationary bike. The system would work by assisting only one leg (the rehabilitating leg) in its pedaling cycle using a motor. The amount of assistance provided by the motor could be adjusted by the user depending on the effort the user is comfortable applying. The motor would turn on during the targeted leg's power phase (phase where the leg pushes down on the pedal), and would turn off for the opposite leg's power phase. This would allow for the opposite leg maintain it's strength without compensating for the other leg, as well as for a constant power output.
The system prototype was programmed using an Arduino Uno and used a 250 W bike motor, load cells, and hall effect sensors as the main components. Data such as the force applied on the pedals and the assistance provided by the motor was displayed on an LCD display. The level of assistance provided by the motor would be set using a potentiometer.

Dressing Aid for Stroke Survivors

For my Biomechanical Engineering Design course, my group and I were assigned a client, March of Dimes Canada, who asked us to design a dressing aid for stroke survivors with difficulty dressing. For the project we focused on upper-body dressing only.
The design solution we developed was an aid that could be hung from the back of a door, with hooks of different sizes and shapes which could be lowered or raised along the frame, which facilitated the porting of garments for the user.
A Finite Element Analysis was conducted on a 3D model of the dressing aid to ensure the materials could withstand the appropriate forces during use, but would fail should a force greater than a certain value be applied that could harm the user.

PURP-LE - Pressure Ulcer Risk Prevention Wearable

For my Bio-Instrumentation project, my group and I developed a wearable device that was intended to alert health care workers of pressure ulcer risk of their patients. The device consisted of four force sensors located on the high risk areas on the upper back, four temperature sensors located near the high risk areas, an accelerometer located on the chest to measure the patient's position, and a Teensy 3.5 microcontroller. The device worked by sensing the duration of force on the high risk areas of the back, the force sensors would act as a the switch. Once force was exerted for a specified amount of time, an alarm would go off, indicating for the patient to be turned. The alarm would be reset once the pressure was relieved for a specified amount of time.
The force sensors that were 'active', the temperature data, as well as the patient position data was all displayed on an app on the caregiver's phone. It would be sent in real-time via a bluetooth connection.
